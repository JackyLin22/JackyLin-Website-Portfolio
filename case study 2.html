Improving the UMD Football Fan Experience

A UX research project exploring how undergraduate students experience UMD football games and identifying opportunities to improve engagement, attendance, and retention.

View Presentation PDF View Research Report

Timeline

September 2024 - December 2024

Role

UX Researcher

Methods

Contextual Inquiry, Ethnography, Affinity Mapping, Personas, Decision Point Models

Overview

As part of a UX research course at the University of Maryland, my team investigated why students attend football games, what keeps them engaged, and what causes them to leave early.

Our goal was to understand how UMD Athletics could improve the undergraduate fan experience through research-driven design solutions.

Research Process

We conducted contextual inquiry interviews and ethnographic field observations with 21 undergraduate students of different majors, grade levels, and interests.

Research included:

  • Contextual inquiry interviews
  • Ethnographic observations
  • Audience immersion
  • Affinity diagramming
  • Interpretation sessions
  • Decision Point Modeling

Key Findings

Students Attended for Social Reasons

Many participants viewed football games primarily as social events rather than sporting events.

Students Frequently Left at Halftime

Long game durations, fatigue, expensive concessions, and low engagement contributed to early departures.

Halftime Entertainment Was Underutilized

Students enjoyed the band and traditions, but many felt disconnected from halftime activities.

Football Knowledge Affected Engagement

Students unfamiliar with football rules struggled to stay invested in the gameplay.

Design Recommendations

Enhanced Halftime Experience

  • QR-code challenges
  • Interactive social engagement
  • Student-focused entertainment
  • Alcohol-free student sections

Gamified Engagement App

  • Attendance tracking
  • Reward systems
  • Interactive polls
  • Ticket management

What I Learned

This project taught me how to synthesize qualitative research into actionable design opportunities.

I learned how ethnographic research reveals behavioral patterns that surveys alone often miss, and how structured synthesis methods can transform observations into meaningful design recommendations.
